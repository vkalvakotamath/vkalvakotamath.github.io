 To general relativists, one nice kink is that SCC always seems to provide an endless number of issues. In particular, a recent paper showed that in semiclassical gravity, the stress-tensor forces quantum backreactions to the curvature terms, which one can use to show inner Cauchy horizon instability. This takes the form,

\[\langle T_{VV} \rangle \sim \frac{C}{V^{2}}, \]

where $V$ is a null Kruskal coordinate centered around $\mathcal{CH}_{\mathcal{I}}$. Due to the nature of $C\sim M, Q, L, \Lambda $, it seems as if this is a nice tool. However, in BTZ spacetime, this has some issues, since $C=0$. This can be corrected by taking nonlinear order quantum backreactions, which, although a quite nerve-wrecking task, can be accomplished. 

For my essay to the 2023 GRF, I wrote a pretty straightforward paper, which has little mathematics and is more or less merely speculative. It suggests that, using the so-called "No Transmission principle", which states that two independent QFTs must be truly independent and must not allow for a transmission of signals, one could obtain, from a gauge/gravity duality, that the corresponding bulk duals must also be independent. A nice illustration is from AdS/CFT, where the bulk regions are the well understood AdS bulks and the corresponding QFT is a CFT living on the boundary. If two CFTs are independent and allow for an AdS bulk correspondence, then the AdS bulks must also be independent. Due to this, SCC can also be found.
